Climate & Health Analysis

Exploring the relationship between climate patterns and disease prevalence across 9 Uganda Districts

Interactive Dashboard

Key Findings

1

Dominance of Malaria

Malaria is the most significant public health burden among the diseases analyzed, accounting for 49.9% of all reported cases (1,448 cases). This highlights its overwhelming priority for healthcare resources and intervention strategies.

2

Seasonal Epidemic Pattern

A clear and pronounced seasonal pattern for malaria is observed. Cases begin to rise in May, peak dramatically in June, and remain high through October. This spike correlates strongly with the rainy seasons.

3

Temperature Range for Peak Transmission

The peak of malaria transmission (5,574 cases) occurs at a maximum temperature of 26.7°C. This temperature falls within the optimal range for mosquito survival and parasite development.

4

Secondary Health Concern

Skin diseases represent the second most common health issue, though their incidence is significantly lower than malaria. This suggests environmental factors also play a major role in population health.

5

Annual Temperature Context

Temperatures are highest in February (reaching a maximum of 30.3°C), which is outside the peak malaria season. This indicates that extremely high temperatures alone are not the primary driver.

Conclusion & Recommendation

The analysis confirms that malaria is a hyper-endemic, climate-sensitive disease in these Ugandan districts. Public health efforts cannot be static; they must be dynamically targeted.

Recommendation

Resources for malaria prevention (e.g., insecticide-treated net distributions, community spraying programs) and clinical preparation (e.g., ensuring clinics are stocked with antimalarial drugs) must be scaled up proactively ahead of the May-October high-risk window. Focus should be greatest in districts with the highest case loads during this period.

Next Step: A deeper analysis could investigate why specific districts have higher incidence than others within the same climate pattern to identify additional social or environmental risk factors.
